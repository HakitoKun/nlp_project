ABSTRACT

Normalizing flows are a class of deep generative models that provide a promising route to sample lattice field theories more efficiently than conventional Monte¬†Carlo simulations. In this work we show that the theoretical framework of stochastic normalizing flows, in which neural-network layers are combined with Monte¬†Carlo updates, is the same that underlies out-of-equilibrium simulations based on Jarzynski‚Äôs equality, which have been recently deployed to compute free-energy differences in lattice gauge theories. We lay out a strategy to optimize the efficiency of this extended class of generative models and present examples of applications.

Introduction

The free energy F is a quantity of central relevance in the description of physical, chemical, or biological systems with a very large (possibly infinite) number of degrees of freedom. For a system in thermal equilibrium at a temperature T, its definition[5] through the equality F‚ÄÑ=‚ÄÑ‚ÄÖ‚àí‚ÄÖTln‚ÄÜZ provides a direct connection between the microscopic physics encoded in the sum of states in the partition function Z and the equation of state describing the macroscopic properties of the system, since in the thermodynamic limit the pressure p equals minus the free-energy density per unit volume. In addition to its importance for low-energy physics systems that traditionally fall into the domain of thermodynamics, the free energy also has applications in high-energy physics, including in elementary particle physics and cosmology. For example, the existence and the nature of phase transitions at very high energies may have an impact on various aspects relevant for the evolution of the early Universe¬†. While the Standard Model predicts smooth cross-overs both in the electro-weak- and in the strong-interaction sector¬†, the existence of first-order phase transitions in various types of extensions of the Standard Model may leave an imprint in gravitational waves¬†.

In quantum chromodynamics (QCD) the theoretical determination of the free energy at temperatures of the order of the hadronic scale necessarily relies on numerical methods, which are based on Wilson‚Äôs lattice regularization¬† and amount to estimating ratios of Feynman path integrals by Markov-chain Monte¬†Carlo calculations‚Äîthat is, differences in free energies. It is well known, however, that typically this involves significant computational costs: commonly used methods to perform such calculations are based on the numerical integration of a derivative with respect to some parameter¬† or on reweighting the field configurations of a simulated ensemble to a target ensemble, specified by different parameter values¬†. The former of these methods, however, introduces a systematic uncertainty due to the discretization of the integration interval; the latter, on the other hand, is often hampered by the fact that the overlap between the most typical configurations in the simulated and in the target ensemble becomes exceedingly small in the thermodynamic limit. The challenging nature of this problem continues to motivate a search for alternative techniques to estimate the free energies in Monte¬†Carlo lattice QCD¬†.

A different computational strategy to evaluate free-energy or effective-action differences has been recently pursued in a series of works¬† that are based on an exact equality in non-equilibrium statistical mechanics discovered by C.¬†Jarzynski more than 25 years ago¬†. It expresses the free-energy difference between two equilibrium states of a statistical system in terms of the exponential average of the work done on the system to push it out of equilibrium. Jarzynski‚Äôs equality is part of a series of works that, during the last decade of the past century, studied in depth the connection between deviations from thermodynamic equilibrium and entropy production¬† (and which are reviewed in refs.¬†): it extends and generalizes earlier results¬† (for a discussion of the connection between those previous works and Jarzynski‚Äôs equality, see refs.¬†), entailing a number of implications, in particular, for the scrambling of quantum information and quantum chaos¬†, while it reduces to known identities in particular limits. Among the implications of Jarzynski‚Äôs equality we mention the fact that, simply using the mathematical properties of convex functions¬†, it is possible to derive from it the inequality that expresses the second law of thermodynamics.

Recently, the explosive growth of machine-learning applications in virtually all fields of human activity has triggered an avalanche of novel implementations of these techniques also in the physical sciences¬† and in elementary particle physics¬†, broadly extending their previous domains of usage¬†. This also includes applications in lattice field theory: as examples of recent works in this area of research, we mention refs.¬†, but this list is likely to grow much longer in the next few years, as the lattice community is developing approaches that are expected to make machine-learning techniques part of the standard lattice-QCD toolbox¬†. A class of deep generative models called normalizing flows¬† represents one of the most active and interesting developments in this area of research¬†. Normalizing flows can be thought as an invertible map between a latent (easy) distribution and the target probability distribution, whose complexity is encoded in the trainable parameters of the neural networks that compose the flow itself. The fact that the configurations sampled using this kind of generative models are statistically independent is a promising feature, as it represents a completely new way to approach a typical problem that plagues Monte¬†Carlo simulations close to the continuum limit, namely the so-called critical slowing down. On the other hand, training times seem to grow very quickly when approaching the continuum limit of the theory of interest¬† so more work is needed to improve the scalability of training procedures. Interestingly, it is crucial to note that normalizing flows do not simply provide a new sampling technique for lattice field theories, but represent also a natural tool for the determination of the partition function Z. Namely, the importance sampling technique introduced in ref.¬† allows for the direct estimation of the free energy at a certain values of the theory parameters, and not just of its difference with respect to another point in parameter space, thus representing a conceptual evolution with respect to the aforementioned Monte¬†Carlo free-energy estimation techniques.

Normalizing flows and non-equilibrium Monte¬†Carlo calculations based on Jarzynski‚Äôs equality are the two main topics that we study in the present work: despite the obvious differences between these two computational approaches, in the following we prove the existence of a direct connection between them, and show how this relation can be fully exploited using a more general class of generative models called stochastic normalizing flows¬†. The structure of our manuscript is the following: after reviewing the basic aspects of Jarzynski‚Äôs equality, and discussing its practical use for Monte¬†Carlo integration in section¬†2, in section¬†3 we reformulate the equality in a framework suitable both for normalizing flows and stochastic processes, starting from the formulation of ref.¬† and reprising previous work on ref.¬†. Next, in section¬†4 we present an example of physical application of these flows in the evaluation of the free energy of the œï‚Å¥ two-dimensional lattice field theory, comparing the effectiveness of different types of flows. Finally, in section¬†5 we recapitulate our findings and discuss possible future extensions of this work.

Jarzynski‚Äôs equality

Consider a statistical-mechanics system, with degrees of freedom œï, whose dynamics depends on a set of parameters collectively denoted as Œ∑ (which can be the couplings appearing in the Hamiltonian H, etc.). Jarzynski‚Äôs equality¬† states that the ratio of the partition functions corresponding to equilibrium states of the system corresponding to two different values of Œ∑, to be denoted as $\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{fin}}}$ a $\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}$, is equal to the exponential average of the work W, in units of the temperature T, that is done on the system, when, starting from thermodynamic equilibrium with parameters $\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}$, it is driven out of equilibrium by a change of its coupling from $\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}$ to $\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{fin}}}$ during a time interval $[t_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}, t_{\mbox{\tiny{fin}}}]$, according to a protocol Œ∑(t):
$$\label{Jarzynski_equality}
\frac{Z_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{fin}}}}}{Z_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}}} = \overline{\exp \left( -W/T \right)}.$$
The average (denoted by the bar) appearing on the right-hand side of eq.¬†([Jarzynski_equality]) is taken over all possible trajectories in the space of configurations that the system can follow, when its parameters are modified according to Œ∑(t). While the starting configurations of these trajectories are equilibrium ones, this is no longer the case for all $t>t_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}$, as during each trajectory the system is driven out of equilibrium and never allowed to relax to equilibrium anymore. As a consequence, eq.¬†([Jarzynski_equality]) describes a non-trivial relation between equilibrium quantities (on the left-hand side) and non-equilibrium ones (on the right-hand side). The time t on which the parameters Œ∑ depend can be either real time or Monte¬†Carlo time. Eq.¬†([Jarzynski_equality]) can be proven in different ways. In appendix¬†6, we review a ‚Äúconstructive‚Äù proof, that is closest to the implementation of Monte¬†Carlo algorithms to compute free-energy differences by evaluating the right-hand side of the equation above. In this case, the time t is discretized and identified with the Monte¬†Carlo time, while the work W can be written as
$$W = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} \left\{ H_{\eta_{n+1}}\left[\phi_n\right] - H_{\eta_n}\left[\phi_n\right] \right\}$$
where Œ∑_(i)‚ÄÑ=‚ÄÑŒ∑(t_(i)).

It may be surprising that the Œ∑(t) protocol, which describes ‚Äúhow‚Äù the parameters of the system are let evolve in time, is fixed and arbitrary, and is not averaged over. As the proof in appendix¬†6 shows, the result of the $\overline{\exp \left( -W/T \right)}$ average appearing on the right-hand side of eq.¬†([Jarzynski_equality]) is independent from Œ∑. From the point of view of a Monte¬†Carlo implementation, however, the choice of Œ∑ has a strong effect on the efficiency with which the algorithm can produce accurate and precise numerical estimates of the $Z_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{fin}}}}/Z_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}}$ with a finite number of trajectories.

To understand the meaning of eq.¬†([Jarzynski_equality]), it is interesting to consider it in two particular limits.

In the limit when the $[t_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}, t_{\mbox{\tiny{fin}}}]$ time interval becomes infinitely long, the Œ∑ parameters evolve infinitely slowly, so that the configurations remain arbitrarily close to thermodynamic equilibrium along each trajectory. Then, the evolution of the system is dissipationless and the work along each trajectory is equal to the free-energy difference between the final and the initial statistical ensembles. In that case, the right-hand side of eq.¬†([Jarzynski_equality]) trivially reduces to exp‚ÄÜ(‚àíŒîF/T).

In the opposite limit, when the switching process from $\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}$ to $\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{fin}}}$ becomes instantaneous, eq.¬†([Jarzynski_equality]) can be written as
$$\label{reweighting}
\overline{\exp \left( -W/T \right)} = \lim_{\tau \to 0} \sum_{\phi_0} \sum_{\phi_1}\pi_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}}\left[ \phi_0 \right] \exp \left( -\frac{H_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{fin}}}}\left[\phi_0\right] - H_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}}\left[\phi_0\right]}{T}\right) P_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{fin}}}}\left[ \phi_0 \to \phi_1 \right].$$
The sum over œï‚ÇÅ is trivial and one is left with:
$$\label{reweighting_bis}
\overline{\exp \left( -W/T \right)} = \lim_{\tau \to 0} \sum_{\phi_0} \pi_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}}\left[ \phi_0 \right] \exp\left( -\frac{H_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{fin}}}}\left[\phi_0\right] - H_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}}\left[\phi_0\right]}{T}\right).$$
The sum on the right-hand side of eq.¬†([reweighting_bis]) can be interpreted as an expression for $Z_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{fin}}}}$ (divided by $Z_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}}$) as a weighted sum over the configurations that contribute to $Z_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}}$, where the weight of each configuration œï‚ÇÄ is $\exp\left( -\left\{H_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{fin}}}}\left[\phi_0\right] - H_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}}\left[\phi_0\right]\right\}/T\right)$: this means that in this limit Jarzynski‚Äôs equality¬†([Jarzynski_equality]) simply reduces to the equation describing statistical reweighting¬†.

We also wish to point out that Jarzynski‚Äôs equality¬†([Jarzynski_equality]) is closely related to another important result in non-equilibrium statistical mechanics, Crooks‚Äô theorem¬†. The latter states that the ratio between the probability density $\mathcal{P}_{\mbox{\tiny{f}}}(W)$ that a ‚Äúforward‚Äù non-equilibrium transformation cost work W, and the probability density $\mathcal{P}_{\mbox{\tiny{r}}}(-W)$ that the opposite (‚Äúreverse‚Äù) transformation cost work ‚ÄÖ‚àí‚ÄÖW is given by
$$\label{Crooks_theorem}
\frac{\mathcal{P}_{\mbox{\tiny{f}}}(W)}{\mathcal{P}_{\mbox{\tiny{r}}}(-W)} = \exp \left( - \frac{\Delta F - W}{T} \right) .$$
The connection between eq.¬†([Crooks_theorem]) and Jarzynski‚Äôs equality is obvious, as eq.¬†([Jarzynski_equality]) can be obtained by multiplying eq.¬†([Crooks_theorem]) by $\mathcal{P}_{\mbox{\tiny{r}}}(-W)$ and integrating over W. Note that an interesting implication of Crooks‚Äô theorem is that the free-energy difference ŒîF is the value of W for which $\mathcal{P}_{\mbox{\tiny{f}}}(W)$ and $\mathcal{P}_{\mbox{\tiny{r}}}(-W)$ are equal.

Finally, we note that the theoretical results presented in this section for a statistical mechanics system are immediately translatable in the language of quantum field theory by substituting H[œï]/T with the Euclidean action S[œï] and W/T with the generalized work
$$\begin{aligned}
\label{eq:w_stochastic}
 w(\phi_0,\phi_1,\dots , \phi_N)
  &= \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} \left\{ S_{\eta_{n+1}} \left[\phi_n\right] - S_{\eta_n}\left[\phi_n\right] \right\} \\
  &= S_{\eta_{N}} \left[\phi_N\right] - S_{\eta_0} \left[\phi_0\right] - Q(\phi_0,\phi_1,\dots , \phi_N)\end{aligned}$$
where in the second line we introduced the quantity
$$\label{eq:Q_stochastic}
 Q(\phi_0,\phi_1,\dots , \phi_N) = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} \left\{ S_{\eta_{n+1}} \left[\phi_{n+1}\right] - S_{\eta_n+1}\left[\phi_n\right] \right\}$$
that is the equivalent of the heat exchanged with the environment during the transformation defined by the protocol Œ∑(t). Thus, eq.¬†([Jarzynski_equality]) can be written as
$$\begin{aligned}
\label{eq:Jarzynski2}
\frac{Z_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{fin}}}}}{Z_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}}} 
 &= \langle \exp \left( -w(\phi_0,\phi_1,\dots , \phi_N) \right)  \rangle_{\mbox{\tiny{f}}} \nonumber \\
 &= \int {\rm{d}}\phi_0  \, {\rm{d}}\phi_1 \dots {\rm{d}}\phi_N \, p_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}}(\phi_0) \, P_{\mbox{\tiny{f}}}[\phi_0,\phi_1,\dots, \phi_N] \, \exp \left( -w(\phi_0,\phi_1,\dots , \phi_N) \right),\end{aligned}$$
where the average over all possible paths $\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}\to \eta_{\mbox{\tiny{fin}}}$ has been expressed through the probability $P_{\mbox{\tiny{f}}}$ of going through a given set of configurations œï‚ÇÄ‚ÄÑ‚Üí‚ÄÑœï‚ÇÅ‚ÄÑ‚Üí‚ÄÑ‚Ä¶‚ÄÑ‚Üí‚ÄÑœï_(N), having used the distribution $p_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}}(\phi_0) = e^{-S_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}}(\phi_0)}/Z_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}}$ to sample œï‚ÇÄ. Let us add that it is inside $P_{\mbox{\tiny{f}}}$ where it lies the dependence of the calculation on crucial details of the transformation, such as the protocol Œ∑(t) or the Monte¬†Carlo algorithm chosen to update the system in the intermediate steps.

We end this section by pointing out that the same equality can be used to compute the expectation value of a generic observable ùí™ at $\eta = \eta_{\mbox{\tiny{fin}}}$:
$$\label{eq:expectation_value_0}
\langle \mathcal{O} \rangle_{\eta=\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{fin}}}} = \frac{\langle \mathcal{O}(\phi_N) \exp(-w(\phi_0,\phi_1,\dots , \phi_N)) \rangle_{\mbox{\tiny{f}}}}{\langle \exp(-w(\phi_0,\phi_1,\dots , \phi_N)) \rangle_{\mbox{\tiny{f}}}},$$
whose derivation follows closely the one of eq.¬†([eq:Jarzynski2]) in the appendix¬†6.

Connection with normalizing flows

In this section, we first review the basics about normalizing flows (mostly following the presentation in ref.¬†), before exposing the relation between normalizing flows and non-equilibrium Monte¬†Carlo simulations based on Jarzynski‚Äôs equality.

Normalizing flows¬† can be interpreted as (a discrete collection of) bijective and differentiable functions interpolating between two different statistical distributions,[6] and provide a natural tool to construct scalable, arbitrarily complex approximations of unknown posterior distributions in variational-inference problems. Starting from functions that map a base (or ‚Äúprior‚Äù) distribution, which is sufficiently simple to be mathematically tractable, to a target distribution, the density of a statistical sample from the latter can be obtained by constructing its counter-image, and multiplying its density by the product of the Jacobians encoding the volume change along the transformation, i.e. the target distribution is the push-forward of the base distribution. The function from the base distribution to the target distribution can be described as a ‚Äúgenerative‚Äù map, as it transforms ‚Äúnoise‚Äù into the feature-rich, physical target distribution. Conversely, the inverse function is a ‚Äúnormalizing‚Äù one, mapping the target distribution into the simpler base distribution.

Normalizing flows can be implemented as neural networks by ‚Äúdiscretizing‚Äù the functions that interpolate between the base distribution q‚ÇÄ and the target distribution p through a composition of invertible layers, labeled by a natural number 0‚ÄÑ‚â§‚ÄÑn‚ÄÑ‚â§‚ÄÑN. If z denotes a variable from the base distribution and g_(Œ∏) is the generative map, one can write:
g_(Œ∏)(z)‚ÄÑ=‚ÄÑ(g_(N)‚àò‚ãØ‚àòg‚ÇÅ‚àòg‚ÇÄ)(z).
We denote the distributions of the intermediate variables y_(n‚ÄÖ+‚ÄÖ1)‚ÄÑ=‚ÄÑg_(n)(y_(n)) as
q_(n‚ÄÖ+‚ÄÖ1)(y_(n‚ÄÖ+‚ÄÖ1))‚ÄÑ=‚ÄÑq_(n)(g_(n)(y_(n)))‚ÄÑ=‚ÄÑq_(n)(y_(n))|detJ_(n)(y_(n))|‚Åª¬π,
where J_(n) denotes the Jacobian matrix associated with the change of variables between the layers with labels n and n‚ÄÖ+‚ÄÖ1. The training of the network can be done by minimizing the Kullback‚ÄìLeibler (KL) divergence between the generated distribution and the target distribution¬†, which is a measure of the similarity between the two probability distributions and can be written as
$$\label{deterministic_Kullback-Leibler_divergence}
\tilde{D}_{\mbox{\tiny{KL}}}(q_N|p) = \int {\rm{d}}\phi \, q_N(\phi) \left[ \ln q_N(\phi) - \ln p(\phi)\right].$$
In a similar fashion with respect to¬†, we introduce a weight function
$$\label{eq:tilde_w}
\tilde{w}(\phi)=\frac{\exp(-S[\phi])}{Z_0 q_N(\phi)},$$
where we added the normalization constant of the q‚ÇÄ distribution in the denominator for reasons that will be clear in the following. The partition function associated with the target probability distribution p(œï) can be simply expressed as
$$\label{eq:Z_neural}
Z = Z_0 \int {\rm{d}}\phi \, q_N(\phi) \tilde{w}(\phi) = Z_0 \langle \tilde{w}(\phi) \rangle_{\phi \sim q_N},$$
where ‚ü®‚Ä¶‚ü©_(q_(N)) denotes the average over the ensemble described by the probability density distribution q_(N). Then, we are able to write the expectation value of a generic observable ùí™ as
$$\label{expectation_value}
\langle \mathcal{O} \rangle = \frac{1}{Z} \int {\rm{d}}\phi \, q_N(\phi) \mathcal{O}(\phi) \tilde{w}(\phi) = \frac{\langle \mathcal{O}(\phi) \tilde{w}(\phi) \rangle_{\phi \sim q_N}}{\langle \tilde{w}(\phi) \rangle_{\phi \sim q_N}}.$$
Note that the right-hand side of eq.¬†([expectation_value]) expresses the expectation value of ùí™ in the target ensemble through a reweighting from the q_(N) ensemble, which, in turn, is obtained combining the sampling from the base distribution with a deterministic flow g_(Œ∏). In particular, the weight function can be rewritten (as a function of y‚ÇÄ) in the form
$$\begin{aligned}
\tilde{w}(y_0) 
&= \exp\left( - \left\{ S[g_\theta(y_0)] + \ln Z_0 + \ln q_0[y_0] - Q\right\} \right) \nonumber \\
&= \exp\left( - \left\{ S[g_\theta(y_0)] - S_0[y_0] - Q\right\} \right),
\label{eq:tilde_w_2}\end{aligned}$$
where in the second equation we inserted q‚ÇÄ[y‚ÇÄ]‚ÄÑ=‚ÄÑexp‚ÄÜ(‚àíS‚ÇÄ[y‚ÇÄ])/Z‚ÇÄ. We also defined the quantity Q, which encodes the variation in phase-space volume accumulated along the flow:
$$\label{eq:Q_deterministic}
Q=\sum_{n=0}^{N-1} \ln \left| \det J_n (y_n) \right| .$$
In practical implementations, Q depends on the network architecture: for example, it is identically zero in frameworks like NICE¬†, while it is generally non-zero for networks based on Real¬†NVP¬†. Let us note also that eq.¬†([expectation_value]) is not the only way to compute expectation values with normalizing flows: a popular alternative consists in generating the configurations using q_(N)(œï) and applying an independent Metropolis‚ÄìHastings algorithm to correct for the difference between q_(N)(œï) and p(œï). The acceptance rate of the Metropolis step provides a measure of the quality of the flow.[7]

Finally, we obtain for eq.¬†([eq:Z_neural]) the following form
$$\label{eq:Z_neural_2}
 \frac{Z}{Z_0} = \langle \exp \left( - \left\{ S[g_\theta(y_0)] - S_0[y_0] - Q \right\} \right) \rangle_{y_0 \sim q_0}.$$

The reader may have noticed the strong similarities between eq.¬†([eq:Z_neural_2]) and eq.¬†([eq:Jarzynski2]), in particular when $S_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}}$ is identified with S‚ÇÄ: this symmetry is not obvious, as the flows used in these computations are purely deterministic in the one case and stochastic in the other. The aim of this section is to generalize both concepts under a common framework, that can describe both deterministic and stochastic transformations and eventually use them together in a single flow.

Following the work of ref.¬†, we start by considering a configuration y‚ÇÄ sampled from the base distribution and we define a forward path as a sequence of configurations (y‚ÇÄ,y‚ÇÅ,‚Ä¶,y_(t)), with t‚ÄÑ‚â§‚ÄÑN. The probability of going through this path can be expressed as the product of the transition probabilities at all intermediate steps:
$$P_{\mbox{\tiny{f}}}[y_0,y_1,\dots,y_t]=\prod_{n=0}^{t-1} P[y_n \to y_{n+1}],$$
so that the probability of reaching a given configuration y_(t) at a generic step t can be expressed by integrating over the initial configuration y‚ÇÄ (sampled from the base distribution q‚ÇÄ) and over all intermediate configurations:
$$\label{reach_yn_in_stochastic_normalizing_flow}
q_t(y_t)=\int {\rm{d}}y_0  \, {\rm{d}}y_1 \dots {\rm{d}}y_{t-1} \, q(y_0) P_{\mbox{\tiny{f}}}[y_0,y_1,\dots,y_t].$$
Another useful quantity is the probability of going through the reverse path (y_(N),y_(N‚ÄÖ‚àí‚ÄÖ1),‚Ä¶,y‚ÇÄ)
$$P_{\mbox{\tiny{r}}}[y_N,y_{N-1},\dots,y_0]=\prod_{n=0}^{N-1} P[y_{N-n} \to y_{N-n-1}],$$
that allows for the definition of the weight function
$$\begin{aligned}
\label{eq:tilde_w_general}
\tilde{w}(y_0,y_1,\dots, y_N)
 &=\frac{Z}{Z_0} \frac{p(y_N) P_{\mbox{\tiny{r}}}[y_N,y_{N-1},\dots, y_0]}{q_0(y_0) P_{\mbox{\tiny{f}}}[y_0,y_1,\dots, y_N]} \\
 &= \exp\left( - \left\{ S[y_N]- S_0[y_0] - Q\right\} \right)\end{aligned}$$
with
$$\label{eq:Q_general_definition}
Q(y_0, y_1, \dots y_N) = \ln \frac{P_{\mbox{\tiny{r}}}[y_N,y_{N-1},\dots,y_0]}{P_{\mbox{\tiny{f}}}[y_0,y_1,\dots,y_N]} =\sum_{n=0}^{N-1} \left( \ln P[y_{n+1} \to y_n] -\ln P[y_n \to y_{n+1}] \right).$$
Note that, in our discussion above, the quantity Q defined in eq.¬†([eq:Q_deterministic]) was a function of y‚ÇÄ only‚Äîa consequence of the deterministic nature of the flow. For flows containing stochastic steps, Q depends on all y_(n), for 0‚ÄÑ‚â§‚ÄÑn‚ÄÑ‚â§‚ÄÑN, i.e. is a function of a particular trajectory, not only of its starting (or of its final) point. The KL¬†divergence can then be interpreted as a ‚Äúdistance‚Äù between the forward and reverse paths that go through the same configurations[8], i.e.
$$\begin{aligned}
\label{eq:general_Kullback-Leibler_divergence}
\tilde{D}_{\mbox{\tiny{KL}}}(q_N|p) 
&= \int {\rm{d}}y_0  \, {\rm{d}}y_1 \dots {\rm{d}}y_N \, q(y_0) P_{\mbox{\tiny{f}}}[y_0,y_1,\dots, y_N] \ln \frac{q(y_0) P_{\mbox{\tiny{f}}}[y_0,y_1,\dots, y_N]}{p(y_N) P_{\mbox{\tiny{r}}}[y_N,y_{N-1},\dots, y_0]} \\
&= - \langle \ln \tilde{w}(y_0,y_1,\dots, y_N) \rangle_{\mbox{\tiny{f}}} + \ln \frac{Z}{Z_0}\end{aligned}$$
while the ratio of partition functions simply becomes
$$\label{eq:Z_final}
\frac{Z}{Z_0} = \langle \tilde{w}(y_0,y_1,\dots, y_N) \rangle_{\mbox{\tiny{f}}}.$$

Both normalizing flows and the stochastic procedure described in section¬†2 emerge naturally within this framework. Normalizing flows are easily recovered by setting
P[y_(n)‚Üíy_(n‚ÄÖ+‚ÄÖ1)]‚ÄÑ=‚ÄÑŒ¥(y_(n‚ÄÖ+‚ÄÖ1)‚àíg_(n)(y_(n)))
for every n, with g_(n)(y_(n)) being the transformation of layer n. In that case, using the fact that
q_(n)(y_(n))P[y_(n)‚Üíy_(n‚ÄÖ+‚ÄÖ1)]‚ÄÑ=‚ÄÑq_(n‚ÄÖ+‚ÄÖ1)(y_(n‚ÄÖ+‚ÄÖ1))P[y_(n‚ÄÖ+‚ÄÖ1)‚Üíy_(n)],
the right-hand side of eq.¬†([eq:Q_general_definition]) reduces to
$$\sum_{n=0}^{N-1} \ln \left[ q_n(y_n)/q_{n+1}(y_{n+1})\right] =\sum_{n=0}^{N-1} \ln \left| \det J_n (y_n) \right|,$$
i.e. to eq.¬†([eq:Q_deterministic]). Similarly, the quantity $P_{\mbox{\tiny{f}}}[y_0,y_1,\dots, y_N]$ appearing in eq.¬†([eq:general_Kullback-Leibler_divergence]) reduces to a product of Dirac distributions, and after integration this definition of $\tilde{D}_{\mbox{\tiny{KL}}}(q_N|p)$ leads to eq.¬†([deterministic_Kullback-Leibler_divergence]).

For the stochastic procedure used in Jarzynski‚Äôs equality, we first introduce the protocol Œ∑(t) to interpolate between the base distribution and the target distribution. As we remarked above, the Œ∑(t) function (or its discretization on the layers specified by an integer-valued label) is largely arbitrary, provided it satisfies the requirements of yielding the parameters of the base and target distributions at the initial and final times, respectively. Given a (discretized) protocol Œ∑, one can construct the sequence of Boltzmann distributions at the n-th step:
$$\label{Boltzmann_distribution_for_lambda_n}
\pi_n[\phi] = \pi_{\eta_n}[\phi] = \frac{1}{Z_{\eta_n}} \exp \left( - S_{\eta_n}[\phi]\right)$$
and construct transition probabilities P(œï‚Üíœï^(‚Ä≤)) satisfying detailed balance (a condition analogous to eq.¬†([Bayes_theorem])):
$$\label{detailed_balance_for_lambda_n}
\frac{P[\phi\to\phi^\prime]}{P[\phi^\prime \to \phi]} = \frac{\pi_{n+1}[\phi^\prime]}{\pi_{n+1}[\phi]}.$$
Using eq.¬†([detailed_balance_for_lambda_n]), eq.¬†([eq:Q_general_definition]) can be rewritten in the form
$$Q(y_0, y_1, \dots y_N) = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} \ln \frac{\pi_{n+1}[y_n]}{\pi_{n+1}[y_{n+1}]},$$
which, when combined with eq.¬†([Boltzmann_distribution_for_lambda_n]) brings us back to eq.¬†([eq:Q_stochastic]). Similarly, the weight wÃÉ defined in eq.¬†([eq:tilde_w_general]) can be rewritten as
$$\label{eq:tilde_w_stochastic}
\tilde{w}(y_0,y_1,\dots , y_N) = \exp \left( - \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} S_{\eta_{n+1}}[y_n]-S_{\eta_{n}}[y_n] \right) = \exp \left( -w(y_0,y_1,\dots , y_N) \right),$$
where in the argument of the exponential we have recognized the work w done on the system during the (y‚ÇÄ,y‚ÇÅ,‚Ä¶,y_(N)) trajectory, as defined in eq.¬†([eq:w_stochastic]). Thus, eq.¬†([eq:Z_final]) is just Jarzynski‚Äôs equality¬†([Jarzynski_equality]) in a form that can be easily translated both in the language of (deterministic) normalizing flows and of Markov¬†Chain Monte¬†Carlo simulations.

It is clear now that nothing prevents us from creating a ‚Äústochastic‚Äù normalizing flow that contains both deterministic coupling layers and stochastic updates. In the following section we review some possible applications and advantages of such a choice.

Application in lattice œï‚Å¥ field theory

We have performed a series of tests in the two-dimensional œï‚Å¥ interacting field theory defined on a lattice Œõ of size L_(t)‚ÄÖ√ó‚ÄÖL_(s), with lattice spacing a. We denote with N_(t)‚ÄÑ=‚ÄÑL_(t)/a and N_(s)‚ÄÑ=‚ÄÑL_(s)/a the number of sites in the temporal and spatial directions respectively and we impose periodic boundary conditions along both of them. The Euclidean action of the theory is defined as
S(œï)‚ÄÑ=‚ÄÑ‚àë_(x‚ÄÑ‚àà‚ÄÑŒõ)‚ÄÖ‚àí‚ÄÖ2Œ∫‚àë_(Œº‚ÄÑ=‚ÄÑ0,‚ÄÜ1)œï(x)œï(x+ŒºÃÇ)‚ÄÖ+‚ÄÖ(1‚àí2Œª)œï(x)¬≤‚ÄÖ+‚ÄÖŒªœï(x)‚Å¥
and the (target) probability distribution is
$$p(\phi) = \frac{1}{Z} e^{-S(\phi)},$$
where Z denotes the partition function:
$$Z = \int \prod_{x \in \Lambda} {\rm{d}}\phi(x) e^{-S(\phi)}.$$

We use three different flows to generate asymptotically correct configurations: standard normalizing flows, stochastic flows (as described in section¬†2) and stochastic normalizing flows (SNF), in which the affine layers that compose a typical normalizing flow are combined with stochastic layers where Monte¬†Carlo updates are performed.

In each kind of flow, we sample the latent variables z from a normal distribution
$$q_0(z) =  \left (\frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \pi \sigma^2}} \right)^{\left| \Lambda \right|} e^{-S_0(z)}$$
with a Gaussian action
$$\label{eq:gauss_action}
 S_0(z) = \sum_{x \in \Lambda} \frac{z(x)^2}{2 \sigma^2}.$$
We set œÉ‚ÄÑ=‚ÄÑ0.5 so that we exactly recover eq.¬†([eq:phi4_action]) with Œ∫‚ÄÑ=‚ÄÑ0 and Œª‚ÄÑ=‚ÄÑ0. This simplifies the protocol that is needed for stochastic flows when interpolating between q‚ÇÄ(z) and p(œï).

The main observable of interest is the free-energy density of the system f‚ÄÑ=‚ÄÑF/L_(s)‚ÄÑ=‚ÄÑF/(aN_(s)). Since
$$F = - T \ln Z = - \frac{1}{N_t a} \ln Z$$
we can look at the dimensionless quantity
$$a^2 f = - \frac{\ln Z}{N_t N_s}.$$

Recalling the definition of a normalizing flow in eq.¬†([eq:norm_flow]), we use as building blocks of the flow the coupling layers g_(i). In order to ensure invertibility and an easy evaluation of the Jacobian, we define the coupling layers by splitting the lattice into two different partitions. A given layer g_(i) leaves one partition unchanged while acting on the other one. More precisely, we use an even-odd partitioning, so that each subsequent configuration y^(i‚ÄÖ+‚ÄÖ1)‚ÄÑ=‚ÄÑg_(i)(y^(i)) can be written as
$$\begin{aligned}
\label{eq:affine_layer}
g_i :
\begin{cases}
 y^{i+1}_{\mbox{\tiny{A}}} = y^{i}_{\mbox{\tiny{A}}} \\
 y^{i+1}_{\mbox{\tiny{B}}} = e^{-s(y^{i}_{\mbox{\tiny{A}}})} y^{i}_{\mbox{\tiny{B}}} + t(y^{i}_{\mbox{\tiny{A}}})
 \end{cases}\end{aligned}$$
thus even sites are left unchanged when A‚ÄÑ=‚ÄÑeven and B‚ÄÑ=‚ÄÑodd (and vice versa for odd sites). We observe that s and t are two neural networks that take as input a configuration and release as output an equally sized configuration. This setup is commonly referred to as an affine layer and is part of the Real¬†NVP architecture¬†.

When building a normalizing flow, it is a desirable feature that it be equivariant under the symmetries of the probability distribution it is going to approximate. In this case, the target probability distribution p(œï) is invariant under œï‚ÄÑ‚Üí‚ÄÑ‚ÄÖ‚àí‚ÄÖœï transformations: to enforce this ‚Ñ§‚ÇÇ symmetry in the flow we require the mapping g_(Œ∏) to be an odd function with respect to z. We do so by choosing neural networks with a hyperbolic-tangent activation function for both s and t in eq.¬†([eq:affine_layer]), and also by taking the absolute value of the output of s. The resulting distribution q_(N)(œï) is then invariant under z‚ÄÑ‚Üí‚ÄÑ‚ÄÖ‚àí‚ÄÖz transformations.

The networks used in this work are shallow, i.e. with a single hidden layer between input and output. We obtained results with two types of networks: fully connected networks with N_(s)‚ÄÖ√ó‚ÄÖN_(t) neurons in the hidden layer and convolutional networks with kernel size 3‚ÄÖ√ó‚ÄÖ3 and one feature map.

Stochastic flows can be thought as a composition of subsequent ‚Äúlayers‚Äù as well, whose structure can be written very similarly to that of affine layers. In this case, the n-th layer is defined by the protocol parameters Œ∑_(n)‚ÄÑ=‚ÄÑŒ∑(t_(n)) that are used to update the system with the action S_(Œ∑_(n)). Exploiting the locality of the action¬†([eq:phi4_action]), we perform an even-odd partitioning: a stochastic layer acts on an intermediate configuration by updating odd (or even) sites using an algorithm that uses as input only even (or odd) sites, which in turn are kept fixed, in a similar fashion as the affine layer of eq.¬†([eq:affine_layer]). While in this work a highly-efficient heatbath algorithm customized for the target distribution of the œï‚Å¥ action has been used, we observe that the Metropolis‚ÄìHastings algorithm can be used in this approach as well.

Finally, a protocol Œ∑(t) has to be set in order to interpolate between the initial and the final action, in this case eqs.¬†([eq:gauss_action]) and ([eq:phi4_action]) respectively. In practice, one has to gradually change the values of the parameters of the theory to interpolate from the prior distribution (Œ∫‚ÄÑ=‚ÄÑ0 and Œª‚ÄÑ=‚ÄÑ0) to the target distribution at the desired values of Œ∫ and Œª. In this work we always followed a linear protocol in all parameters and for each layer we applied only one heatbath update. We stress, however, that different protocols, such as non-linear ones or with multiple Markov-chain Monte¬†Carlo updates in the same layer, are possible, as well as the possibility to let the intermediate parameters be tunable whenever the flow undergoes a training procedure.

Having fixed the details of affine and stochastic parameters, any flow used in this work is simply characterized by the number of stochastic and deterministic layers: using even-odd partitioning in both cases, we have always used ‚Äúblocks‚Äù of two subsequent layers where both even and odd sites are updated once. In the following we denote the number of affine blocks as $n_{\mbox{\tiny{ab}}}$ and the number of stochastic blocks as $n_{\mbox{\tiny{sb}}}$. In the case of stochastic normalizing flows, where both $n_{\mbox{\tiny{ab}}}\neq 0$ and $n_{\mbox{\tiny{sb}}}\neq 0$, stochastic blocks are always inserted equally distanced between affine blocks so to maximize the number of deterministic layers between them. For example, in the case $n_{\mbox{\tiny{ab}}}= 2 n_{\mbox{\tiny{sb}}}$, the flow is built alternating two affine blocks and one stochastic block.

In the case of normalizing flows and SNFs, we perform the training procedure needed to tune the parameters of the neural networks contained in the affine layers of eq.¬†([eq:affine_layer]) by minimizing the loss function $-\langle \ln \tilde{w} \rangle_{\mbox{\tiny{f}}}$, which equals the KL divergence ([eq:general_Kullback-Leibler_divergence]) minus the ratio Z/Z‚ÇÄ. In order to evaluate the convergence of the training (i.e. the ability of the latent distribution q_(N)(œï) to describe the target distribution p(œï)), we also monitor the variance of the loss
$$\mbox{Var}_{\mbox{\tiny{f}}} (\mathcal{L}) = \mbox{Var}_{\mbox{\tiny{f}}} (-\ln \tilde{w}),$$
and the effective sample size (ESS)
$$\label{eq:ESS}
\mbox{ESS} = \frac{\langle \tilde{w}\rangle_{\mbox{\tiny{f}}}^2}{\langle \tilde{w}^2 \rangle_{\mbox{\tiny{f}}}}$$
which is always in the range [0,1] and tends to 1 for a ‚Äúperfect‚Äù training. These quantities can be calculated for a stochastic flow as well, using the same definitions, and determine the quality of the protocol chosen for the flow.

All results discussed in the rest of this section have been obtained on a NVIDIA Volta V100 GPU with 16GB of memory, used both for training deterministic flows and SNFs and to take measurements for all three types of flows. For all architectures the training was performed by applying 10‚Å¥ steps of gradient descent on the loss using batches of 8000 configurations, while the number of independent measurements was fixed to be $N_{\mbox{\tiny{meas}}}=2\times 10^5$.

Fully connected networks show little to no improvement when more stochastic blocks are inserted, while CNN-based flows improve with increasing $n_{\mbox{\tiny{sb}}}$ at a similar rate even for larger volumes.

The neural networks s and t of eq.¬†([eq:affine_layer]) used in this work can be considered shallow, since a single hidden layer is present. However, in this setup the overall architectures are ‚Äúdeep‚Äù in the sense that the number of affine blocks is relatively large ($n_{\mbox{\tiny{ab}}}\geq 6$). What we found through careful experimentation is that flows with large $n_{\mbox{\tiny{ab}}}$ appear to be the easiest to integrate whenever many stochastic layers are inserted. For example, flows with deeper neural networks or less affine blocks were generally less amenable to improvements in training when trained with many stochastic layers.

At this point however, we point out that a full quantitative comparison between normalizing flows (or SNFs) and stochastic flows, while extremely interesting for future practical applications, is beyond the scope of this work. Such a comparison would depend on a wide variety of factors. For example, including the training time in the overall effort to reach a given error on f is not completely straightforward. Only neural networks strictly need a training procedure to work properly, while for purely stochastic flows a reasonably efficient protocol can be set manually. Then, one has to take into account the dependence on the architecture of affine layers (e.g. the value of $n_{\mbox{\tiny{ab}}}$ or the type of neural networks), on the Monte¬†Carlo algorithm of choice and on the hardware, too. GPUs allow for greater parallelization but, for example, the dependence on the batch size is not obvious when both Markov-chain updates and forward passes on affine coupling layers are performed. We limit ourselves to observe that in the standard and stochastic normalizing flows used in this work the training time was of the order of hours. This is not negligible at all when compared with the time needed to perform $N_{\mbox{\tiny{meas}}}=2\times 10^5$ measurements, which ranges from seconds for flows containing zero or few stochastic layers, to at most a few minutes for flows with $n_{\mbox{\tiny{sb}}}> 100$.

It is interesting to investigate the effects of an increase in the spatial volume of the lattice: heuristically, the target distribution becomes more sharply peaked and thus a better training and/or a more expressive flow are needed in order to reach the same effectiveness in sampling p(œï).

For what concerns stochastic flows, a simple strategy allowing to sample larger volumes as effectively as smaller ones consists in increasing the number of Monte¬†Carlo updates of the protocol. Intuitively, the corresponding transformation becomes closer and closer to a reversible one, i.e. a transformation in which every intermediate point is (almost) at equilibrium. This can be seen in fig.¬†6, where for all volumes the effective sample size grows with $n_{\mbox{\tiny{sb}}}$.

The question is whether this strategy can be implemented structurally for stochastic normalizing flows as well. As we already pointed out, an improvement in the effectiveness of SNFs can be obtained simply adding deterministic and stochastic blocks in an equal manner, roughly keeping $n_{\mbox{\tiny{ab}}}= n_{\mbox{\tiny{sb}}}$ and alternating one block of even-odd affine layers with one block of even-odd Monte¬†Carlo updates. Conclusions and future prospects

In this manuscript we have proven the connection between normalizing flows and non-equilibrium Monte¬†Carlo calculations based on Jarzynski‚Äôs equality (‚Äústochastic flows‚Äù), which becomes apparent when both are used for the determination of the free energy F. In general, stochastic flows by themselves represent an efficient method to sample a target distribution and to directly evaluate the partition function Z: they provide a novel framework to compute expectation values in lattice field theory from first principles and represent a viable alternative to the traditional Monte¬†Carlo approach. In Markov-chain Monte¬†Carlo simulations, measurements are performed on subsequent equilibrium configurations that are part of the same Markov chain, thermalized according to the target coupling(s). The cost of generating a new configuration is as low as the application of a single update on a lattice. However, configurations belonging to the same Markov chain are correlated with each other, reducing the number of effectively independent configurations, and this problem can become potentially severe in the proximity of critical points; in addition, every new set of couplings requires a new chain to be thermalized again. By contrast, in stochastic flows, each measurement is independent from the others, but involves several Monte¬†Carlo updates that ‚Äúinterpolate‚Äù from the prior distribution to the distribution defined by the target coupling(s). The computational cost of this might seem very high at first, due to the relatively large number of Monte¬†Carlo updates needed for convergence. However, it is crucial to note that, as a byproduct of this procedure, all intermediate couplings can be sampled at the same time. It is then clear that this method is particularly suitable when a fine scan of the parameter space of a theory is required. A typical example is the study of thermodynamics in strongly interacting quantum field theories. Indeed, a full-scale numerical study using this approach has already been reported: it is the high-precision computation of the equation of state in the SU(3) Yang-Mills theory in 3‚ÄÖ+‚ÄÖ1 dimensions¬†. In that case, the only parameter changed by the protocol is the inverse coupling Œ≤, which in turn also controls the temperature of the system. The major difference with respect to the stochastic flows used in this work is that the generator of starting configurations was not a treatable (e.g. normal) prior distribution, but a distribution obtained from a Markov chain thermalized at a certain value Œ≤‚ÇÄ of the inverse coupling.

We also showed that a common framework can describe in a natural way both stochastic and normalizing flows, following the work of ref.¬† and expanding it so to explicitly include stochastic transformations based on Jarzynski‚Äôs equality. The construction of stochastic normalizing flows is a direct consequence of this connection: a ‚Äúhybrid‚Äù flow combining deterministic and stochastic transformations proved to be highly efficient at sampling the target distribution, with relatively simple and short training procedures and a limited number of Monte¬†Carlo updates. Our previous work on stochastic flows suggested that increasing the number of intermediate steps, while expensive per se, is an efficient way of improving the effectiveness of the flow, and this is confirmed by the numerical tests performed in this work. However, it was not clear whether this strategy would work at all for stochastic normalizing flows. Interestingly, this proved to be true also in the latter case and with surprisingly high efficiency. We stress though, that this was observed in a rather specific setup in which a) affine blocks based on CNNs are used, b) stochastic and affine blocks are placed in an alternating order, and c) the number of stochastic and affine blocks is increased roughly in the same manner.

The strong similarities between purely stochastic flows and SNFs suggest that the success of the former in full-scale numerical calculations can be replicated with the latter using similar strategies. Moreover, due to the existence of an explicit protocol determined by the stochastic updates, the role of intermediate affine layers can be interpreted straightforwardly. As pointed out above, each stochastic layer can be used to sample the intermediate parameters defined by the protocol Œ∑(t): the (deterministic) affine layers inserted between them are then trained to ‚Äúglue‚Äù together the various steps of the protocol without resorting to further (generally more expensive) intermediate Monte¬†Carlo updates.

Before concluding, we would like to point out that the training of SNFs is not necessarily more efficient in general. A standard normalizing flow is intuitively more ‚Äúfree‚Äù to seek the best possible path between the prior and the target distributions. On the other hand, stochastic normalizing flows are ‚Äúconstrained‚Äù by the protocol chosen for Monte¬†Carlo updates; these intermediate steps happen at fixed values in the parameter space of the theory, which the training is ‚Äúforced‚Äù to go through. Naively, one could expect that a standard normalizing flow will eventually outperform a stochastic one, given the same neural-network architecture; however, this might not happen in a reasonable training time, and a fixed protocol might lead in some instances to a faster training. More work is needed, for example, to understand how the training times needed by SNFs to reach a plateau in the loss function behave when changing the volume of the system.

Among possible directions of future work, our primary interest is to study the effectiveness of SNFs in systems close to criticality, in order to develop the most suitable strategy for SNFs in this region of the parameter space of a theory. More generally, an analysis of the interplay between Monte¬†Carlo updates and different types of neural-network architectures would be highly insightful and could help one understand what exactly the neural networks are learning when ‚Äúcoupled‚Äù to Monte¬†Carlo algorithms in this way. Natural extensions of this work include the use of convolutional architectures for gauge equivariant flows¬†, rational quadratic splines¬† and continuous equivariant flows¬†.

Derivation of Jarzynski‚Äôs equality for Monte¬†Carlo algorithms

We first set our notation. For a system in thermodynamic equilibrium at temperature T, the statistical distribution of the œï configurations is the Boltzmann distribution œÄ:
$$\label{Boltzmann_distribution}
\pi[\phi] = \frac{1}{Z} \exp \left( - H[\phi]/T \right).$$
The partition function Z is related to the free energy F via
Z‚ÄÑ=‚ÄÑexp‚ÄÜ(‚àíF/T).
Let P[œï‚Üíœï^(‚Ä≤)] denote the normalized conditional probability of a transition from a configuration œï to a configuration œï^(‚Ä≤) which defines the Markov-chain algorithm of the Monte¬†Carlo simulation under consideration. At equilibrium, the Boltzmann distribution has to be stationary: the probability that the system evolves from some configuration œï to a given configuration œï^(‚Ä≤) must be equal to the probability that it evolves from œï^(‚Ä≤) to some other configuration, i.e.
‚àë_(œï)œÄ[œï]P[œï‚Üíœï^(‚Ä≤)]‚ÄÑ=‚ÄÑ‚àë_(œï)œÄ[œï^(‚Ä≤)]P[œï^(‚Ä≤)‚Üíœï].
A sufficient (albeit not necessary) condition to enforce the validity of eq.¬†([Boltzmann_distribution_stationarity]) is to assume that the summands, not only the sums, are equal:
œÄ[œï]P[œï‚Üíœï^(‚Ä≤)]‚ÄÑ=‚ÄÑœÄ[œï^(‚Ä≤)]P[œï^(‚Ä≤)‚Üíœï],
i.e. the detailed-balance condition.

Let us consider an out-of-equilibrium evolution of the system during the time interval from $t_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}$ to $t_{\mbox{\tiny{fin}}}$, denoting the $t_{\mbox{\tiny{fin}}}-t_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}$ difference as Œît, and assuming that this time interval is divided into N subintervals (which we take to be of equal width œÑ‚ÄÑ=‚ÄÑŒît/N, for the sake of simplicity), setting $t_n=t_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}+ n \tau$ for integer 0‚ÄÑ‚â§‚ÄÑn‚ÄÑ‚â§‚ÄÑN. We identify the discrete time steps t_(n) with the steps in Monte¬†Carlo time in a Markov-chain algorithm.

Finally, let us introduce the quantity ‚Ñõ_(N)[œï] defined as
$$\label{discretized_exponential_work}
\mathcal{R}_N[\phi] = \exp \left( - \frac{1}{T}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1} \left\{ H_{\eta_{n+1}}\left[\phi_n\right] - H_{\eta_n}\left[\phi_n\right] \right\}\right),$$
which represents the sum of the exponentiated work (divided by T) done on the system during each of the time intervals of width œÑ, when the couplings are switched from Œ∑_(n) to Œ∑_(n‚ÄÖ+‚ÄÖ1). In the N‚ÄÑ‚Üí‚ÄÑ‚àû limit, ‚Ñõ_(N)[œï] tends to the quantity that is averaged over on the right-hand side of eq.¬†([Jarzynski_equality]). Using eq.¬†([Boltzmann_distribution]), ‚Ñõ_(N)[œï] can be rewritten in terms of the Boltzmann distribution as
$$\label{discretized_exponential_work_Z_pi_ratios}
\mathcal{R}_N[\phi] = \prod_{n=0}^{N-1} \frac{Z_{\eta_{n+1}} \pi_{\eta_{n+1}}\left[\phi_n \right]}{Z_{\eta_n} \pi_{\eta_n}\left[\phi_n \right]},$$
so that the average of eq.¬†([discretized_exponential_work_Z_pi_ratios]) over all possible trajectories from $t_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}$ to $t_{\mbox{\tiny{fin}}}$ can be written as
$$\label{averaged_discretized_exponential_work_Z_pi_ratios}
\overline{\exp \left( -W/T \right)} = \lim_{N \to \infty} \sum_{\left\{ \phi_n \right\}_{n=0}^N }\pi_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}}\left[ \phi_0 \right] \prod_{n=0}^{N-1} \left\{ \frac{Z_{\eta_{n+1}}}{Z_{\eta_n}} \cdot \frac{\pi_{\eta_{n+1}}\left[\phi_n\right]}{\pi_{\eta_n}\left[\phi_n\right]} \cdot P_{\eta_{n+1}}\left[ \phi_n \to \phi_{n+1} \right] \right\},$$
having used the fact that the system is initially in thermal equilibrium, hence the probability distribution for the configurations at $t=t_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}$ is given by eq.¬†([Boltzmann_distribution]), and having denoted the N‚ÄÖ+‚ÄÖ1 sums over configurations at $t_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}$, t‚ÇÅ, t‚ÇÇ, ‚Ä¶, t_(N‚ÄÖ‚àí‚ÄÖ1), $t_{\mbox{\tiny{fin}}}$ as
‚àë_({œï_(n)}_(n‚ÄÑ=‚ÄÑ0)^(N))‚ÄÑ=‚ÄÑ‚àë_(œï‚ÇÄ)‚àë_(œï‚ÇÅ)‚àë_(œï‚ÇÇ)‚Ä¶‚àë_(œï_(N‚ÄÖ‚àí‚ÄÖ1))‚àë_(œï_(N)).
The product of ratios of partition functions in eq.¬†([averaged_discretized_exponential_work_Z_pi_ratios]) simplifies to $Z_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{fin}}}}/Z_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}}$. Moreover, using eq.¬†([detailed_balance]), the sum appearing on the right-hand side of eq.¬†([averaged_discretized_exponential_work_Z_pi_ratios]) can be rewritten as
$$\label{simplified_discretized_exponential_work_pi_ratios}
\overline{\exp \left( -W/T \right)} = \frac{Z_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{fin}}}}}{Z_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}}} \lim_{N \to \infty} \sum_{\left\{ \phi_n \right\}_{n=0}^N }\pi_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}}\left[ \phi_0 \right] \prod_{n=0}^{N-1} \left\{\frac{\pi_{\eta_{n+1}}\left[\phi_{n+1}\right]}{\pi_{\eta_n}\left[\phi_n\right]} \cdot P_{\eta_{n+1}}\left[ \phi_{n+1} \to \phi_n \right] \right\}.$$
In the latter expression, the ratios of Boltzmann distributions simplify to $\pi_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{fin}}}}\left[ \phi_N \right]/\pi_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}}\left[ \phi_0 \right]$, which, in turn, simplifies against the $\pi_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}}\left[ \phi_0 \right]$ factor:
$$\label{discretized_exponential_P_product}
\overline{\exp \left( -W/T \right)} = \frac{Z_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{fin}}}}}{Z_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}}}  \lim_{N \to \infty} \sum_{\left\{ \phi_n \right\}_{n=0}^N }\pi_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{fin}}}}\left[ \phi_N \right] \prod_{n=0}^{N-1} P_{\eta_{n+1}}\left[ \phi_{n+1} \to \phi_n \right].$$
The sum over the initial configurations can be performed explicitly, as œï‚ÇÄ appears only in the P_(Œ∑‚ÇÅ)[œï‚ÇÅ‚Üíœï‚ÇÄ] term, and the result is 1, due to the normalization of the conditional transition probability. Next, the same argument can be repeated to sum over the œï‚ÇÅ, œï‚ÇÇ, ‚Ä¶, œï_(N‚ÄÖ‚àí‚ÄÖ1) configurations. Finally, noting that also $\pi_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{fin}}}}\left[ \phi_N \right]$ is normalized to 1, one obtains
$$\label{final_discretized_exponential_P_product}
\overline{\exp \left( -W/T \right)} = \frac{Z_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{fin}}}}}{Z_{\eta_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}}},$$
which is eq.¬†([Jarzynski_equality]).

We remark that, although in this proof we used the Boltzmann distributions at all times $t_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}\le t \le t_{\mbox{\tiny{fin}}}$, we did this only to re-express the exp‚ÄÜ(‚àíH/T) terms appearing in eq.¬†([discretized_exponential_work]). The configurations œï_(n) at $t > t_{\mbox{\tiny{in}}}$ are not in thermal equilibrium. Moreover, for simplicity, we assumed the temperature T to be constant throughout the evolution of the system along each trajectory, but this does not necessarily have to be the case¬†.
